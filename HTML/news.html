In Kathmandu, 55,830 people took the first dose of Verosel

In Kathmandu, a total of 55,830 people have been vaccinated against Kovid on Tuesday and Wednesday. According to the Health Office, Kathmandu, a total of 48 vaccination centers have been vaccinated in the district, including 44 previously scheduled and four added on Wednesday. Health Office Kathmandu Chief Badri Bahadur Khadka said that 12,372 people were vaccinated from some vaccination centers on Tuesday and 43,458 people from 48 vaccination centers on Wednesday. Successful second liver transplant at Chitwan Medical College

Chitwan, 31 July. This is the second successful liver transplant at Chitwan Medical College (CMC) amid the Corona epidemic. The second successful liver transplant was performed at CMC on July 3. CMC had earlier performed the first liver transplant on April 25. According to CMC, 47-year-old Bharat Bikram Shahi of Jajarkot in Kushe Village Municipality-9 underwent a liver transplant after taking 15 hours. Shahi was donated a liver by his wife Kamal Devi Shahi, 48. Specialist Surgeons of the College Surgeons Dr. Sanjay Kumar Yadav, Dr. Ekananda Singh, Dr. Jayant Kumar Sah, Hepatology Dr. Sagar Poudel, Dr. Barun Shrestha, Dr. Kiran Adhikari in Anesthesia and Critical Care, Dr. Navin Yadav, Dr. Suresh Gautam, Dr. Virendra Raut, Radiologist Dr. Deepak Adhikari, Dr. Subash Ojha was the participant. Similarly, staff nurses Kamala Chhetri, Priyanka Poudel, Salina Tiwari, Sumi Karki, Barsha Lohani, Pratima Regmi, Asmita Bhandari, Sudipa Devkota, Kriti Chaudhary, Anju Thapa, Shruti Yadav, Nitu Basnet and Sushma Bhujel. A team including Anita Bhandari was a participant. Dr. Nirbha Goyal's team from India also participated in the transplant. Dr. Ekananda Singh informed that 60 percent of Mrs. Shahi's liver was transplanted to her husband for transplant. He also said that Mrs. Shahi was discharged on July 12 after her health improved. He informed that the health condition of the transplanted Bharat Bikram Shahi is also improving. He said he would be discharged next week if the pace improved. Dr. Singh also said that the transplanted liver and the liver cut for transplantation will increase to 90 to 95 percent within the next two to three months.

1.5m doses of single-shot J&J vaccines arrive from US

As many as 1.5 million doses of Johnson & Johnson Covid-19 vaccine have arrived here in the capital.A flight ferrying the single-shot vaccine vials provided by United States government to Nepal landed in the Tribhuvan International Airport Monday morning, as per the Ministry of Health and Population. Authorities have moved the consignment to Teku based vaccine storage facility managed by the Department of Health Services.As per preliminary reports, individuals falling in the age group 50-55 will receive the jabs. However, the government is yet to make an official announcement regarding the same.US government dispatched the said amount of jabs to Nepal under COVAX facility, and in line with US President Joe Biden's pledge to donate a batch of 80 million US-made vaccines globally to counter vaccine disparity. Nepal so far has received and conducted the inoculation drive with COVISHIELD, developed by Oxford AstraZeneca and manufactured by Serum Institute of India and Vero Cell developed by Sinopharma and Beijing Institute of Biological Products.This will be the third of the Covid-19 vaccine to be used in Nepal and the first of the single-shot kind.The country that is hard-pressed to control the spread of the contagion under the second wave of coronavirus infection has the only option of vaccinating as many as its citizens if it were to put up any kind of fight against a possible third wave of the pandemic.

COVID-19 Origins Still a Mystery

Scientists using computer modelling to study SARS-CoV-2, the virus that caused the COVID-19 pandemic, have discovered the virus is most ideally adapted to infect human cells - rather than bat or pangolin cells, again raising questions of its origin. In a paper published in the Nature journal Scientific Reports, Australian scientists describe how they used high-performance computer modelling of the form of the SARS-CoV-2 virus at the beginning of the pandemic to predict its ability to infect humans and a range of 12 domestic and exotic animals. Their work aimed to help identify any intermediate animal vector that may have played a role in transmitting a bat virus to humans, and to understand any risk posed by the susceptibilities of companion animals such as cats and dogs, and commercial animals like cows, sheep, pigs and horses.
